def query_url(key,fieldnames,filterstring,sortvar,numpages):
    """
    This takes all of the necessary parameters to form a query 
    to college scorecard
    Input: key (data.gov API key, string), fieldnames (list of variable 
      names), filterstring (string for filtering), sortvar (variable to 
      sort with by descending), numpages (number of pages to return)
    Output: url (string)
    """
    base_url = 'https://api.data.gov/ed/collegescorecard/v1/schools.json' 
    fnames = ','.join(e for e in fieldnames) #join fieldnames list, separated bt comma
    sym = ['?', '&_fields=', '&_page=', '&_per_page=100', '&_sort=', ':desc&api_key='] #list of various symbols 
    #forming the final url piece by piece, adding the necessary syntaxical elements.
    url = (base_url + sym[0] + filterstring + sym[1] + fnames + sym[2] + str(numpages) + sym[3] + sym[4] + 
    sortvar + sym[5] + key)
    return url

It seems that for the most part we are seeing that debt amounts are heavily concentrating around the 17,000 to 27,000 range and with a few to either extremes of the debt spectrum. This is evident in all of the scatter plots. Perhaps, this is an amount deemed reasonable or acceptable to the general student population across these different universities. Otherwise, most of the associations seems to be as expected. For example, median household income and federal loan rate tend to trend upward with increasing debt. Both types of tuitions, on the other hand, exhibit suprising associations with debt. For both, the distribution look quite strange, not adhereing to any obvious patterns. It is also far from obvious or clear that as tuition goes up, debt goes up with it. The highest tuitions are probably private univsersities where, perhaps, the type of students who goes there can afford it while maintaining the same, mid-range amount of debt. Plotting median household income vs. in-state tution reveals that for higher end tutions universities, median household income is also at the upper end.
